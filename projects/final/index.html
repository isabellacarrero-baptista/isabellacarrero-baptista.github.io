Crash course on Maryland car crashes

Impaired driving causes high number of deaths

By Isabella Carrero-Baptista

The total 172 car crash deaths in Maryland this year grew by two this morning, when a Toyota RAV4 overturned at the ramp from Maryland Route 80 to northbound Interstate-270. The driver and passenger died at the scene.

Since 2016, the Maryland Highway Safety Office reports that about a total of 4,300 drivers have died at the wheel of a car. Yet at a glance, Maryland still remains safe compared to national data.

Forbes ranked Maryland ninth in the Top 10 Safest States for Drivers, accounting for fatal car accidents per capita, total people killed in car accidents per capita and reasons for the crash. Overall, the state ranked 43rd.

However, a high percentage of those fatalities were from drivers under the influence of alcohol and/or drugs. Impaired driving comprised 75 percent of the total deaths, ranking 13th among the other states. Wicomico County had the greatest percentage of impairment fatalities, making up 31 percent of its total 93 deaths since 2016.

Montgomery County — which was tied at fifth with Prince George’s County, Charles County and Cecil County — has placed an extra emphasis on reducing impaired driving through Maryland’s Vision Zero law.

The bill passed in 2019 with the goal of reducing motor vehicle-related deaths or serious injuries by 2030, according to the MHSO. Sweden first introduced this initiative in 1997 amid countrywide rising traffic fatalities and serious injuries.

Maryland’s Strategic Highway Safety Plan targets six major causes of motor vehicle crashes and fatalities, called “Emphasis Areas”: distracted driving, impaired driving, infrastructure, occupant protection, pedestrians and bicyclists, speed and aggressive driving.

“Under the Zero Deaths Maryland umbrella, these agencies use a data-driven and interdisciplinary strategy that applies education, enforcement, engineering, and emergency medical services to prevent fatal and severe crashes,” states the 2021-2025 SHSP.

Montgomery County supplemented the initiative with its Ending Impaired Crash Fatalities Action Plan. According to the plan, impaired driving deaths account for nearly one-third of all traffic deaths in the county. A contributing factor to the issue is the legalization of marijuana in the state, which was allowed to be legally be used for recreational purposes in July, 2023. A study in the Journal of Studies on Alcohol and Drugs estimated the legalization would cause a 6.5 percent increase in car crash injury rates and a 2.3 percent increase in fatal crash rates.

Young people are most at risk of impaired driving. Almost three in 10 drivers with blood alcohol concentration levels of .08%, the legal limit, were between the ages of 25 and 34 years old in 2016. The plan also reported that the second largest group were ages 21 to 24, which made up 26 percent.

There have been other local efforts to reduce the risk of youth driving under the influence, such as Safe and Sane. This event, usually run by high schools’ Parent Teacher Student Association, is a sober all-night celebration after graduation.

“Parents can go home knowing their senior is in a safe and fun environment without the risks associated with the statistically deadliest night for young adults,” said the Oakdale High School Safe and Sane Graduation Committee.

Despite these efforts, the total state fatalities have recently increased. There was an eight percent increase from 2022 to 2023, the biggest jump since 2016, when the MHSO started collecting traffic data.

“The number of serious and fatal injuries remains higher than we can accept,” said Montgomery County Executive Marc Elrich. “Vision Zero is an audacious goal. It is one that we can all embrace and champion together.”
